Prior Knowledge 👀: Before starting this investigation, it was known that plants require water, light, and carbon dioxide to grow. Plants use a process called photosynthesis to produce their own food. This process occurs in chloroplasts, which contain chlorophyll. Chlorophyll gives plants their green colour and is responsible for absorbing light energy. It was understood that light is important for plant growth; however, it was unclear whether different colours of light affect growth in different ways. Sunlight is known to be composed of multiple colours combined, but the effect of individual light colours on plant development had not been explored.

Background Information ℹ️: Germination is the process in which a seed wakes from dormancy (a temporary state of suspense, growth or development in an organism) and begins to grow into a new plant, normally initiated by the absorption of water and the development of a sprout. We have chosen mung beans in our experiment because they are unique in that they do not require soil to grow, but can instead be sprouted in just a few days using only water, darkness, and a container. Plants then grow by carrying out photosynthesis, a process in which light energy is used to convert water and carbon dioxide into glucose, providing energy for growth. This process occurs in chloroplasts and relies on chlorophyll, which is the green pigment in chloroplasts that absorbs light energy to convert into chemical energy. Visible light consists of different wavelengths on the electromagnetic spectrum, which are seen as colours (ROYGBIV). White light contains all wavelengths, while coloured filters allow only specific wavelengths to pass through. This is the reason why we perceive red objects as “red” as the filter absorbs the rest of the coloured wavelengths and only lets the red wavelength through. Chlorophyll absorbs light most strongly in the red and blue regions of the light spectrum, making these colours the most effective for photosynthesis. Blue light supports strong leaf and root development, while red light is highly effective for photosynthesis and promotes plant growth. In contrast, green light is mostly reflected by plants and is therefore less effective, while yellow light is also less effective when used on its own. In this experiment, materials were carefully selected to control light exposure. Opaque cardboard was used in the black setup to block all light, as black PVC film may still allow some light to pass through. Transparent PVC film was used in the white setup to allow the full light spectrum to reach the plants without alteration as a control. The remaining samples were set up with red, yellow, green and blue films individually.

Procedure ☀️:
Thirty mung beans were soaked in one container containing 100 mL of water for 1 hour in a cupboard.
2 sheets of paper towel were placed into a petri dish and spread evenly to cover the bottom whilst the mung beans were still being soaked.
Water was added until the paper towel was fully moist but not flooded, using the same amount of water (10 mL) in each dish also whilst the mung beans were being soaked.
After soaking, four mung beans were placed evenly spaced on top of the paper towel.
Steps 2–4 were repeated to prepare a total of 6 identical petri dishes.
Six boxes were prepared and labelled: blue, red, green, yellow, white, and black.
Coloured PVC film was placed over each box according to its label (blue, red, green, yellow, or white).
The six boxes were placed on a windowsill in order to absorb the most sunlight evenly.
The black box was covered completely with opaque cardboard to block all light.
The white box was left with no coloured film so all light could pass through.
Five small holes were poked on two sides of each box for ventilation.
One petri dish was placed inside each box.
All boxes were placed next to each other on the same windowsill so they received the same amount of sunlight.
On each measurement day (Monday, Tuesday, Thursday, and the following Monday), the height of each plant was measured in centimetres (cm) from the base of the bean to the tip of the sprout using a ruler before watering.
On each measurement day, the same amount of water (10 mL) was added to each dish to keep the paper towel moist.
After 7 days, the average height of the plants in each dish was calculated and recorded in a table.
